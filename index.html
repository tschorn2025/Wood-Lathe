Segmented Turning
For: collectors, design enthusiasts, special occasions
Segmented turning builds a bowl or vessel from dozens — sometimes hundreds — of individually cut wood pieces glued together before turning. The result is a precisely engineered pattern of contrasting woods, grain directions, and colors that a solid blank could never produce. It demands accuracy at every stage: segment angles must sum perfectly, glue-ups must be flat and square, and the turner must think through the final form long before the lathe starts. A deeply rewarding discipline that sits at the intersection of woodworking, design, and mathematics.

Wood Selection Guide
Timber Properties
+
Every timber used in these projects has its own personality — how it sources, how it behaves under a tool, and how it responds to a finish. This guide covers the species most relevant to gift turning: sourcing expectations, workability on the lathe, and finishing characteristics.

Maple
Acer saccharum / A. rubrum
Domestic
Sourcing
Hard maple (sugar maple) is widely available across North America from hardwood lumber dealers, online retailers, and turning-blank suppliers. Sold as both kiln-dried boards and ready-cut blanks. Expect reliable grain consistency; figured maple (curly, bird's eye) commands a premium and is best sourced from specialist suppliers or turning shows.
Workability
A dense, tight-grained hardwood that turns cleanly with sharp tools. It resists tearing but can burn quickly if tools are dull or feed rate slows — keep tools sharp and keep moving. Excellent for fine detail and crisp coves or beads. End grain is particularly smooth when skewed cleanly. Hard maple dulls tools faster than softer species; a quick hone between passes pays off.
Finishing
Takes virtually any finish beautifully. Its pale, neutral colour is ideal for oil finishes (tung, Danish), lacquer, and shellac — all of which add a warm honey tone. Sands to a glassy surface; go through the grits to 400 before finishing. Food-safe finishes (mineral oil, beeswax) work well for kitchen items. Maple accepts dye evenly, which is an advantage if colour is desired.
Used in: Rolling Pin · Wooden Spoons · Drawer Knobs · Bottle Stoppers · Gift Box Set

Cherry
Prunus serotina
Domestic
Sourcing
American black cherry is widely available from hardwood dealers throughout the eastern US and online. Typically sold kiln-dried in boards or turning blanks. Grain is generally straight and consistent; occasional mild figure occurs naturally. Relatively affordable for a premium hardwood. Green (freshly cut) cherry from orchard prunings is also excellent for turning and often free from local orchards or arborists.
Workability
One of the most pleasant domestic hardwoods to turn. Medium density with a fine, straight grain that cuts cleanly and crisply. Rarely catches or tears under a sharp tool. Responds well to skew chisels and spindle gouges. Produces fine shavings and minimal dust. A forgiving choice for intermediate turners working on lidded boxes or bowls — the grain telegraphs mistakes less than open-grained species.
Finishing
Cherry's great quality is its patina: it starts a pale pinkish-brown and deepens dramatically to a rich reddish-amber over months of light exposure. Any clear finish — oil, lacquer, hard wax — accelerates and preserves this patination. Avoid heavy pigmented stains, which mask cherry's natural colour evolution. Sands easily to 320–400 grit. A wiping varnish or Danish oil gives a warm, hand-rubbed appearance well-suited to gift pieces.
Used in: Drawer Knobs · Lidded Storage Boxes · Honey Dipper · Gift Box Set

Walnut
Juglans nigra
Domestic
Sourcing
Black walnut is available from most hardwood dealers and many online turning blank suppliers across the US. Kiln-dried boards are standard; air-dried slabs and bowl blanks are common at regional mills or woodturning clubs. Walnut crotch and figured pieces are prized for bowls and hollow forms. More expensive than maple or cherry but readily accessible. Note: some individuals have a walnut wood allergy (juglone sensitivity) — worth mentioning for food-contact items.
Workability
An excellent turner with moderate density and good chip clearance. Sharp tools produce clean, almost silky cuts. The grain can be mildly interlocked, which may cause slight tearout on end grain — a light cut and sharp edge resolves this. Walnut dust is a known sensitiser; always use a dust mask and ensure good ventilation. The dark heartwood and pale sapwood create striking contrast in segmented work or if left together intentionally.
Finishing
Walnut has a naturally rich, dark chocolate colour that looks superb under virtually any clear finish. Danish oil or raw linseed oil deepens and enriches the colour with minimal effort. Hard wax oil gives a durable, satin sheen well-suited to boxes and bowls. Avoid water-based finishes, which can raise grain and produce a slightly grey cast. Walnut is among the easiest species to finish beautifully — even a simple oiled surface is impressive.

Oak
Quercus alba / Q. robur
Domestic
Sourcing
Both white oak (US) and English oak (UK) are among the most widely stocked hardwoods in the world. Available from virtually every hardwood dealer in board or blank form. Often the most affordable of the premium hardwoods. Red oak is equally common but coarser in grain — white oak is generally preferred for turned pieces due to its tighter pores. Air-dried oak from local mills or estate trees is also an excellent and sometimes free source.
Workability
Oak is harder and heavier than cherry or walnut and requires sharper tools and more deliberate technique. Its open grain means tearout is a real risk — light finishing cuts and a well-honed edge are essential. The medullary rays (visible as flecks on quartersawn faces) add striking visual interest but can chip at the tool edge if cutting speed is too slow. Less commonly used for fine spindle work but excellent for bowls and platters where its character shines.
Finishing
Oak's open grain requires a grain filler or sanding sealer for truly smooth surfaces, or a deliberate open-grain aesthetic with an oil finish. Danish oil, hard wax oil, and shellac all work well. Oak reacts with iron — avoid steel wool during finishing, as tannic acid in the wood causes black staining. White oak accepts ebonising (black iron vinegar) dramatically, which is a useful decorative option. Fuming with ammonia darkens oak to a beautiful tobacco colour used in Arts and Crafts furniture.
Used in: Bowls · Platters · Memorial Pieces

Ash
Fraxinus americana / F. excelsior
Domestic
Sourcing
White ash (North America) and European ash are widely available as lumber and turning blanks. Ash has become increasingly available at low cost due to the devastation of emerald ash borer in North America, making fallen urban and suburban ash trees a plentiful, often free source. Sourcing locally from tree services is highly recommended — much of the ash being felled would otherwise be chipped. Kiln-dried boards are available at most hardwood dealers.
Workability
Ash is tough, elastic, and moderately easy to turn. Its open, ring-porous grain is more pronounced than cherry or maple, which means some tearout on cross-grain cuts — keep tools sharp and make light finishing passes. Ash turns quickly and cleanly once technique is dialled in. Its flexibility and strength make it ideal for handles and utilitarian pieces. Spalted ash (partially decayed, with black zone lines) is particularly beautiful and worth seeking out.
Finishing
Ash has a strong grain figure that reads beautifully under penetrating oil finishes. Danish oil, tung oil, and hard wax oil all enhance the contrast between early and late wood. Like oak, its open grain may benefit from a sanding sealer before film finishes. Ash takes colour well — ebonising or liming (white filler rubbed into open grain) produce dramatic effects suited to contemporary gift pieces. Sands to a smooth surface if working with fine grits.
Used in: Handles · Platters · Segmented Work · Memory Pieces from fallen trees

Padauk
Pterocarpus soyauxii
Exotic
Sourcing
African padauk is sourced from equatorial West Africa (Cameroon, Nigeria, Congo). Available from specialist exotic timber dealers, online retailers, and woodturning suppliers in the US, UK, and Australia. Typically sold as small turning blanks or pen blanks due to its popularity for accent work. Relatively affordable among exotic species. Look for CITES-compliant sourcing — padauk is not currently restricted but sourcing from responsible dealers is good practice.
Workability
Padauk is moderately dense with interlocked grain and turns surprisingly well for an exotic. It cuts cleanly with sharp tools but can produce fine, abrasive dust — always wear a respirator, as padauk dust is a known sensitiser and may cause skin irritation. Its interlocked grain can cause occasional tearout; light, supported cuts help. The vivid red colour makes even small accent pieces visually striking, and it is particularly popular in segmented turning for colour contrast.
Finishing
Padauk's vibrant scarlet-red colour is its defining characteristic — but it fades on exposure to UV light over time, settling to a deep russet-brown. A UV-inhibiting finish (oil-based urethane or an outdoor-rated lacquer) slows this transition. Oil finishes applied wet appear to deepen the red dramatically. Sand through 400 grit for a smooth base. Avoid water-based finishes, which can cause raised grain and blotchy colour. The colour change is irreversible but produces a handsome result in its own right.
Used in: Pill Organizer · Segmented Pieces · Accent Bands · Pen Blanks

Purpleheart
Peltogyne spp.
Exotic
Sourcing
Purpleheart is native to tropical Central and South America (Brazil, Guyana, Suriname). Available from most exotic hardwood dealers and online turning blank suppliers. Sold in boards, bowl blanks, and pen blanks — pen blanks are the most economical entry point. Moderately priced for an exotic. The colour varies in intensity by board; selecting stock in person at a dealer allows better colour matching for segmented work.
Workability
A very dense, hard timber that dulls tools quickly. Carbide-tipped tools or frequent sharpening is recommended. It can be brittle and prone to checking if dried too quickly or forced. Despite its density it turns to a smooth surface with care. Dust is a sensitiser — respiratory protection is important. Its hardness makes it excellent for accent bands in segmented turning where fine, crisp lines are required. Avoid overheating at the tool edge, which will cause the wood to turn brown (heat-reversible by sanding).
Finishing
Fresh-cut purpleheart is a dull brown-grey that transforms to vivid violet-purple on exposure to air — this happens within minutes and is one of its most satisfying qualities. Exposure to UV and light will eventually fade it to a dark brownish-purple; UV-inhibiting finishes slow this. Oil finishes and shellac both enhance the colour. Keep finished pieces away from prolonged direct sunlight. Sands well but generates heat quickly — work through grits with moderate pressure only.
Used in: Pill Organizer · Segmented Boxes · Accent Rings · Pens

Pine
Pinus strobus / P. sylvestris
Domestic
Sourcing
Eastern white pine (US) and Scots pine (UK/Europe) are among the most available softwoods in the world. Found at every timber merchant, big-box store, and sawmill. Turning-grade pine is best sourced from hardwood dealers or specialist suppliers — construction-grade pine is often too knotty and resinous. Old-growth salvaged pine (from barns, floors, beams) turns beautifully and is worth seeking from architectural salvage yards.
Workability
Pine is soft, light, and turns quickly — but its softness is a challenge. Dull tools compress and tear rather than cut; extremely sharp tools and a confident feed rate are essential. Knots are almost impossible to turn cleanly and should be avoided in blanks. Pine's resin can load sandpaper quickly; use fresh paper and avoid over-sanding. Best suited to larger, simpler forms — fine detail is difficult. Excellent as a learning species for new turners due to low cost and availability.
Finishing
Pine is resinous, which can bleed through film finishes. De-resin with acetone or mineral spirits before finishing if resin pockets are present. Shellac makes an excellent sealer coat before lacquer or varnish. Oil finishes on pine can remain tacky — wax-based finishes or lacquer are often more reliable. Pine's pale, creamy colour and visible growth rings give it a casual, rustic quality. Paint and milk paint take beautifully on pine for decorative turned items.
Used in: Practice Blanks · Decorative Turnings · Painted Items · Salvage Memory Pieces

Ebony
Diospyros spp.
Exotic
Sourcing
True ebony (Gabon, Macassar, Ceylon varieties) is one of the world's most heavily regulated timbers. Gabon ebony (D. crassiflora) is CITES Appendix II — commercial trade requires documentation. Source only from reputable dealers who provide legal provenance. Available in small blanks, pen blanks, and off-cuts from specialist exotic wood suppliers. Due to its scarcity and regulation, use ebony as an accent species — finials, small details, pull knobs — rather than for large blanks.
Workability
Exceptionally dense and hard — one of the heaviest commercial timbers. Extremely sharp tools are non-negotiable; even slightly dull edges will burnish rather than cut, producing heat and a poor surface. Light cuts at moderate speed work best. Its brittleness means edges can chip — support cuts carefully near thin sections. Ebony dust is toxic and a known sensitiser; a high-quality respirator and dust extraction are essential. Despite the challenges, ebony turns to an extraordinarily smooth, lustrous surface.
Finishing
Ebony is unique in that it often requires no finish at all — its dense, oily surface can be burnished to a high gloss with shavings or leather during the final spinning pass. If a finish is desired, wax or a hard wax oil is ideal. Avoid oil finishes that may remain tacky on ebony's oily surface. Ebony's natural lustre and jet-black colour are its own finish — the goal is to protect without obscuring. A light buffing with carnauba wax produces a jewel-like result.
Used in: Finials · Accent Details · Pull Knobs · Pen Components

Red Cedar
Thuja plicata / Juniperus virginiana
Domestic
Sourcing
Two distinct species go by this name. Western red cedar (Thuja plicata) comes from the Pacific Northwest and is widely available from lumber yards and hardwood dealers across North America. Eastern red cedar (Juniperus virginiana) is more commonly found at regional sawmills, from tree services, or as salvage — it is rarely stocked at big-box stores. Both are affordable. Eastern red cedar in particular is available free or cheaply from rural fence-post clearing or land management. Look for straight-grained, knot-free sections for turning blanks.
Workability
Red cedar is a softwood, which means sharp tools and a confident hand are especially important — dull edges will compress and fuzz rather than cut cleanly. Western red cedar is lighter and more brittle; thin sections can split unexpectedly. Eastern red cedar is denser and more forgiving, turning to a surprisingly smooth surface. Both species are highly aromatic — the pleasant scent is a selling point for gift pieces like closet balls or drawer inserts. Work in a well-ventilated area as the aromatic oils can irritate the respiratory tract with prolonged exposure.
Finishing
Red cedar's natural oils make it somewhat resistant to penetrating finishes — test on offcuts first. A light coat of danish oil or pure tung oil enhances the rich red and cream colour contrast while preserving the natural aroma. Film finishes like lacquer or shellac seal in the scent, which may be desirable (or not) depending on the end use. For aromatic gift items such as sachets or closet inserts, leave the surface unfinished or lightly waxed to allow the oils to breathe. Sands easily but raises grain readily — dampen and re-sand before final grit.

Ironwood
Ostrya virginiana / Olneya tesota
Domestic
Sourcing
"Ironwood" covers several exceptionally hard, dense species. Eastern hophornbeam (Ostrya virginiana) is found across eastern North America and sourced primarily from sawmills, tree services, or directly from felled urban trees — it is rarely stocked at timber dealers due to its small diameter and regional nature. Desert ironwood (Olneya tesota) is found in the Sonoran Desert and, as a protected species, may only be legally harvested as salvage from naturally fallen trees — source with care and documentation. Both are most readily obtained by knowing local arborists or woodturning clubs who have access to felled material.
Workability
Ironwood is among the hardest and densest timbers in North America — it will sink in water. This density is both its challenge and its appeal. Carbide tools are strongly recommended; HSS tools dull almost immediately and require constant honing. Take light, deliberate cuts at moderate speed. The wood turns to an extraordinarily smooth, almost self-burnishing surface due to its density. It produces fine, heavy shavings and abrasive dust — a respirator is essential. Thin sections are extremely strong and hold crisp detail beautifully. Despite the difficulty, the results are worth the effort: ironwood pieces have a weight and solidity that feel genuinely precious.
Finishing
Ironwood's extreme density means it accepts very little finish by penetration — surface finishes sit on top rather than soaking in. A hard wax oil or paste wax applied to the spinning piece and buffed produces an exceptional natural lustre that rivals ebony. Oil finishes may remain tacky; test before committing to a full application. The wood's inherent colour — ranging from pale cream-grey in hophornbeam to rich dark brown in desert ironwood — is its own decoration. Sanding through to 600 grit and then burnishing with a paper bag or leather strop produces a surface that genuinely does not require further finishing.
Used in: Handles · Mallet Heads · Small Bowls · Accent Details · Heirloom Pieces
